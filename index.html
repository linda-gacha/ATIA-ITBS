1. What Are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. They come in a variety of sizes and compositions, ranging from gas giants like Jupiter to rocky planets similar to Earth. As of now, over 5,000 exoplanets have been confirmed, with thousands more candidates awaiting verification .

2. The Importance of Exoplanet Research

Studying exoplanets helps scientists understand the formation and evolution of planetary systems, including our own. It also provides insight into the potential for life elsewhere in the universe. Researchers focus on identifying habitable zones—regions around stars where conditions might be suitable for life, primarily based on the presence of liquid water .

3. Notable Discoveries

Kepler-186f: Discovered by NASA’s Kepler mission, Kepler-186f is the first Earth-sized planet found in the habitable zone of another star. Its discovery ignited interest in potentially habitable worlds beyond our solar system . TRAPPIST-1 System: This system consists of seven Earth-sized planets, three of which are located in the habitable zone. The TRAPPIST-1 planets have garnered attention due to their potential for hosting life and their relatively close proximity to Earth .

4. The Future of Exoplanet Exploration

The future of exoplanet exploration looks promising, with upcoming missions focusing on direct imaging of exoplanets and their atmospheres. Understanding the composition of these planets could help scientists identify those with the potential to support life .
